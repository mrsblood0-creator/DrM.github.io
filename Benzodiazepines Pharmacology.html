Benzodiazepines Pharmacology: Mechanism of Action, Therapeutic Uses & Adverse Effects

Overview of benzodiazepines, their mechanism of action, clinical uses, adverse effects, and toxicity management.

Overview

  • Benzodiazepines are sedative hypnotic drugs. Other sedative hypnotics include barbiturates, Z-drugs, and other hypnotics.
  • They largely replaced barbiturates in the treatment of anxiety disorders, insomnia, and seizures because they are safer and more effective.
  • Chlordiazepoxide was the first benzodiazepine, discovered in 1955 by chemist Leo Sternbach.

Sedative and Hypnotic Effects

  • Sedative effect: reduction in CNS activity, leading to reduced anxiety and a calming effect.
  • Hypnotic effect: further reduction in CNS activity, leading to drowsiness and sleep.
  • Benzodiazepines can achieve both effects depending on the dose.

Benzodiazepines Classification

Type Examples Duration T1/2
Ultra short acting Remimazolam 30 min 30 min
Short acting Midazolam, Triazolam 1 h 2 h
Intermediate acting Alprazolam, Lorazepam, Temazepam, Oxazepam, Nitrazepam 6–8 h 5–20 h
Long acting Diazepam, Clonazepam, Flurazepam 8–14 h 50–100 h

Pharmacokinetics

  • Absorption: Oral formulations are well absorbed with good bioavailability.
  • Distribution: Highly lipid soluble, distribute to most body tissues including the brain, then rapidly redistribute to muscle and fat, decreasing plasma and brain concentrations. Redistribution terminates central effects.
  • Metabolism: Mainly in the liver by oxidation into active metabolites and conjugation with glucuronic acid to form inactive metabolites. Lorazepam undergoes conjugation directly without oxidation.
  • In liver disease, old age, and with microsomal enzyme inhibitors, oxidation is greatly reduced while conjugation is less affected. Lorazepam is preferred in these cases.
  • Excretion: Renal.

Mechanism of Action

  • Benzodiazepines act on GABAA receptors in the CNS (like barbiturates and Z-drugs).
  • GABA receptors are pentameric (five subunits) and are the chief inhibitory receptors in the CNS.
  • Normally activated by GABA neurotransmitter, leading to opening of chloride channels, reducing neuronal excitability and inhibiting nerve transmission (CNS inhibition).
  • Benzodiazepines enhance GABA action by interacting with the alpha and gamma subunits of GABAA receptors, increasing chloride ion flow, resulting in hyperpolarization and CNS depression.
  • Benzodiazepines increase the frequency of chloride channel opening in GABAA receptors, but only in the presence of GABA. If GABA is absent, benzodiazepines have no effect (unlike barbiturates, which work even if GABA is absent).

Pharmacologic Effects

  • Antianxiety effect at small doses.
  • Sedative and hypnotic effects at larger doses.
  • Anesthetic effect with midazolam IV.
  • Euphoria, hallucinations, dizziness, and paresthesia.
  • Anticonvulsant effect (clonazepam for absence seizures).
  • Anterograde amnesia: inability to remember events during drug action.
  • Muscle relaxation.

Therapeutic Uses

  • First line for cessation of seizure activity and status epilepticus in emergency departments (especially midazolam, lorazepam, diazepam).
  • Short-term management of generalized anxiety disorder, panic attacks, and some phobias (SSRIs are used for long-term management).
  • Treatment of insomnia (second line after Z-drugs).
  • Clonazepam for myoclonic and absence seizures.
  • Short surgical procedures.
  • Safe and effective for short-term use only (< 6 weeks); long-term use leads to tolerance, dependence, and cognitive impairment.

Adverse Effects

  • Tolerance and physical dependence (decreased GABA sensitivity, increased excitatory transmitters such as NMDA receptors).
  • Rebound insomnia after discontinuation.
  • Hangover: psychomotor depression the day after use of long-acting drugs.
  • Memory disturbance.
  • Respiratory depression and respiratory arrest.
  • Drowsiness and confusion.
  • Headache.
  • Nausea and vomiting.
  • Double vision.
  • In neonates, rarely causes laryngospasm, bronchospasm, ventricular arrhythmias, and syncope.

Contraindications

  • Severe respiratory insufficiency.
  • Sleep apnea syndrome.
  • Acute narrow angle glaucoma (increases IOP).
  • Pregnancy (teratogenic): use only if benefit outweighs risk.
  • Use with other CNS depressants (e.g., alcohol).

Use in Renal and Hepatic Impairment

  • Hepatic impairment: Lorazepam is the safest option.
  • Renal impairment: Decreased clearance; lorazepam may be safe in ESRD but dose adjustment is needed.

Toxicity

  • Benzodiazepines can be abused; overdose may occur intentionally or due to hepatic/renal problems.
  • Toxicity symptoms: CNS depression (loss of consciousness, shallow breathing).

Treatment of Toxicity

  • Flumazenil is the antidote for benzodiazepine toxicity (may cause withdrawal seizures if given in high dose).
  • Management of airway, breathing, and circulation (ABC).
  • IV fluids.
  • Activated charcoal is contraindicated.
