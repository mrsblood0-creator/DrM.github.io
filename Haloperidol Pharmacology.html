Haloperidol Pharmacology: Mechanism of Action, Therapeutic Uses & Adverse Effects

Overview

  • Famous trade name is Haldol
  • It is classified as high potency typical antipsychotic drug from butyrophenones family
  • Used in treatment of schizophrenia and some other mental disorders
  • Haloperidol was discovered in 1958 by the Belgian physician Paul Janssen

Pharmacokinetics

  • Available as oral, IM and IV formulas
  • Good absorption of oral formula with bioavailability of 60-70%
  • Distribution: haloperidol is heavily protein bound by 90%, free part distributed to different body tissues
  • Metabolized by the liver through glucuronidation and CYP oxidation by CYP3A4
  • Excreted through bile and urine

Mechanism of action

  • Haloperidol work by antagonizing dopamine D2 receptors in the brain
  • It blocks dopamine in the mesolimbic and mesocortical pathway which lead to relieving the psychotic symptoms
  • It also blocks dopamine in the nigrostriatal pathway which lead to extrapyramidal symptoms as adverse effects
  • It also blocks dopamine in the tuberoinfundibular pathway lead to hyperprolactinemia as an adverse effect
  • Haloperidol also has anti serotonin, antimuscarinic and anti histamine activity
  • It blocks alpha adrenergic receptors to some degree too

Therapeutic uses

  • Psychotic disorders like schizophrenia and acute psychosis
  • Tics in Tourette syndrome
  • Manic phase of bipolar disorder
  • Agitation and aggressive behavior
  • Alcohol withdrawal
  • Treatment of intractable hiccups
  • Severe nausea and vomiting not responding to first line medications

Dosing

  • For schizophrenia and Tourette syndrome: dose is 0.5 to 2 mg orally 2 to 3 times a day, max dose is 30 mg/day
  • For acute agitation: 2 to 5 mg intramuscular every 4 to 8 hours

Treatment considerations

  • In long term use of haloperidol for schizophrenia and other chronic disorders, daily dose should be reduced to the lowest level possible for maintenance
  • In long term use of this medication, routine monitoring including measurement of BMI, BP, fasting blood sugar, and lipid profile is recommended due to risk of side effects

Adverse effects

  • Extrapyramidal symptoms (movement disorders): those are more common than with use of other antipsychotics
  • Acute dystonia: muscle spasm develops within hours to days after initiation
  • Akathisia: restlessness develops days to months after initiation
  • Tardive dyskinesia: uncontrollable repetitive movements develops after years of initiation
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome
  • Muscle rigidity
  • Hyperthermia
  • Autonomic disturbance
  • Anticholinergic side effects: elevated temperature, dry mouth, sedation, constipation and urinary retention
  • Weight gain
  • Hyperprolactinemia which lead to erectile dysfunction in males, amenorrhea, galactorrhea in females
  • It also block alpha adrenergic receptors to some degree leading to postural hypotension, reflex tachycardia and palpitation
  • Eye Lens opacities if haloperidol used for long time , because it accumulates in the lens
  • QT interval prolongation => torsades de pointes

Contraindications

  • Previous hypersensitivity reaction in the individual
  • Parkinsonâ€™s disease
  • Lewy body dementia
  • CNS depression, coma patients, concurrent use with barbiturates, benzodiazepines and opioids

Warning

  • There is increased risk of death if haloperidol is used in elderly patient with dementia related psychosis, that is why it is not FDA approved for this use
  • IV use with higher doses may lead to torsades de pointes arrhythmia and sudden death
  • Motor instability, orthostatic hypotension may lead to falls and fractures while on this medication

Haloperidol toxicity

  • Symptoms of toxicity include
    • Severe extrapyramidal symptoms
    • Hypotension
    • Sedation
    • Coma with severe respiratory depression
  • There is no antidote for Haloperidol and treatment is supportive
  • Gastric lavage and activated charcoal
  • ABCs, endotracheal intubation and ventilation
  • IV fluids, albumin and vasopressors

Drug Interactions

  • Amiodarone: both prolong QT interval => torsades de points
  • Amphetamine and methylphnidate: haloperidol antagonize the action of norepinephrine and dopamine in patients taking these medications
  • Epinephrine: haloperidol antagonize eipinephrine leading to decrease BP
  • Levodopa: antagonize
  • Lithium: rare cases of encephalopathy if these medications used together
  • Methyldopa: increased extrapyramidal side effects
  • CYP inducers and inhibitors affect metabolism of haloperidol: inducers such as carbamazepine , phenobarbital and rifampicin associated with low levels of haloperidol, inhibitors such as quinidine, buspirone and fluoxetine associated with higher plasma levels of haloperidol
  • Tricyclic antidepressant metabolism decreases if used with haloperidol together and this might lead to toxicity
