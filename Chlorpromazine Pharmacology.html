Chlorpromazine Pharmacology: Mechanism of Action, Therapeutic Uses & Adverse Effects

Overview, pharmacokinetics, mechanism, uses, dosing, and adverse effects of chlorpromazine.

Overview

  • Famous trade names: Thorazine and Largactil
  • Classified as low potency typical antipsychotic drug from phenothiazines family
  • Used in treatment of schizophrenia and some other mental disorders
  • Chlorpromazine was developed in 1950 and it was the first antipsychotic

Pharmacokinetics

  • Chlorpromazine is available as oral, IM, IV formulas
  • Oral absorption is variable with bioavailability of 10-90%
  • Widely distributed through the body, high lipid solubility so it easily crosses blood brain barrier to CNS
  • Extensively metabolized in the liver by CYP enzymes especially CYPA12 and 2D6, also metabolized by kidney and GIT
  • Excreted in urine

Mechanism of Action

  • Chlorpromazine works by antagonizing dopamine D2 receptors in the brain
  • Blocks dopamine in the mesolimbic and mesocortical pathway → relieves psychotic symptoms
  • Blocks dopamine in the nigrostriatal pathway → extrapyramidal symptoms as adverse effects
  • Blocks dopamine in the tuberoinfundibular pathway → hyperprolactinemia as an adverse effect
  • Also has anti-serotonin, antimuscarinic, and anti-histamine activity
  • Blocks alpha adrenergic receptors to some degree too

Therapeutic Uses

  • Treatment of psychosis including schizophrenia
  • Manic phase of Bipolar disorder
  • Treatment of chronic hiccups
  • Treatment of nausea and vomiting not responding to first line medications (anti-emetic effect from blocking dopamine, histamine, and muscarinic receptors in vomiting center)
  • Treatment of agitation and severe anxiety
  • Off label: migraine and serotonin syndrome

Dosing

  • For schizophrenia
    • Oral: start 25-75 mg/day orally twice per day, maintain at 200 mg/day, max 800 mg/day
    • IM/IV: 25 mg then 50 mg after 1-4 hours, repeat, dose can be 300-800 mg/day
  • For nausea and vomiting
    • Oral: 10-25 mg every 4-6 hours as needed
    • IM/IV: 25-50 mg every 4-6 hours as needed

Adverse Effects

  • Extrapyramidal symptoms (movement disorders): tremor, dyskinesia, akathisia (less than other typicals)
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome
  • More anticholinergic side effects (blurred vision, dry mouth, sedation, constipation)
  • Blocks alpha adrenergic receptors → postural hypotension
  • Mild bone marrow suppression
  • Increased prolactin → decreased libido, gynecomastia, galactorrhea, erectile dysfunction

Contraindications

  • Previous hypersensitivity reaction to the drug
  • CNS depression, coma
  • Bone marrow suppression
  • Hepatic failure and acute liver disease
  • Pheochromocytoma

Cautions

  • Risk of closed angle glaucoma in elderly patients taking this medication
