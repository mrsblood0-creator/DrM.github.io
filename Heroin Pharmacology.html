Heroin Pharmacology: Mechanism of Action, Therapeutic Uses & Adverse Effects

  • Heroin (is a trade name) is also known as diacetylmorphine and diamorphine (acetylated morphine) and it is a Semi synthetic opioid
  • Heroin is used medically in several countries to relieve pain (analgesic) but it is banned in most countries because it is highly addictive
  • It is used illegally for recreational use due to the euphoria it induces

History

  • First synthesized in 1874 by Charles Alder Wright an English chemist but the invention didn’t lead to any further developments
  • Re synthesized again in 1897 by Felix Hoffmann who is a German chemist, heroin marketed initially as an over the counter drug for cough suppression to replace the addictive morphine
  • Soon in 1910 heroin had one of the highest addiction among its users
  • In 1924, United States banned heroin

Pharmacokinetics

  • Administration: oral and IV are the most common formulas
  • Absorption: the oral form is very well absorbed which then undergoes extensive first pass effect via deacetylation to be converted to morphine
  • Distributed quickly to tissues especially the brain
  • Metabolized by liver and excreted through kidney and liver

Mechanism of action

  • heroin works on the opioid receptors which are three major receptors Mu (μ), delta (δ) and kappa (κ) which are available everywhere in the body , especially in the central nervous system

Pharmacologic effects

  • Heroin produces greater pharmacologic effects than the morphine
  • Activation of these opioid receptors lead to several central effects (CNS effects):
    • Analgesia
    • Euphoria
    • Sedation
    • Respiratory depression
    • Cough suppression
    • Miosis
    • Nausea and vomiting
    • Pruritus
  • Activation of these opioid receptors lead to several peripheral effects including:
    • CVS
    • GIT
    • RS
    • Renal
    • Endocrine
    • Immune

Adverse effects

  • Tolerance
  • Physical dependence
  • Addiction
