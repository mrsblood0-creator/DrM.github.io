Nefopam (Acupan): Mechanism of Action, Therapeutic Uses & Adverse Effects

Overview

  • Scientific name is Nefopam, trade name is Acupan
  • Non opioid, centrally acting analgesic medication
  • It was originally synthesized in 1960

Pharmacokinetics

  • Available in oral and IV formulations
  • Oral formula is well absorbed with low bioavailability
  • After absorption it is bound by 70% to plasma proteins are the rest is distributed to the tissues, especially brain where it exert its effects
  • Metabolized by the liver and excreted through urine and bile
  • Elimination half life is 3 to 8 hours

Mechanism of action

  • Nefopam act on the brain to relive pain through inhibition of the reuptake of serotonin, norepinephrine and dopamine, it also reduce glutamate signaling
  • It has no antipyretic or anti inflammatory activity

Therapeutic uses

  • It is used to treat moderate to severe pain
  • it is more potent analgesic than aspirin
  • Compared to morphine, 20mg of nefopam gives about same analgesia as 10 mg of morphine and there is no risk of respiratory depression in nefopam
  • Nefopam is also used to combat severe hiccups
  • It is used for prevention of shivering during surgery or recovery from surgery

Adverse effects

  • Sweating, dizziness and nausea especially at higher doses
  • Precipitation of seizures in epilepsy patients (thus it is contraindicated)
  • Urinary retention
  • Headache
  • Tachycardia (aggravate angina)
  • Rash

Contraindications

  • Convulsive disorders
  • MI
  • It is also contraindicated in patients who are being treated with monoamine oxidase inhibitors and tricyclic antidepressant because there is a risk for serotonin syndrome or hypertensive crisis

Overdose

  • Overdose manifest as convulsions, hallucinations, tachycardia and hypertension
  • Nefopam overdose may lead to death
  • Treatment is supportive by limiting drug absorption by activated charcoal and managing the tachycardia (with beta blockers)
