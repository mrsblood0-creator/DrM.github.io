Barbiturates Pharmacology: Mechanism of Action, Therapeutic Uses & Adverse Effects

Overview of barbiturates, their mechanism of action, clinical uses, adverse effects, and toxicity management.

Overview

  • Barbiturates are sedative hypnotic drugs. Other sedative hypnotics include benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, and other hypnotics.
  • Barbiturates are dangerous because they may cause respiratory and vasomotor depression.
  • Amylobarbitone was the first barbiturate discovered in 1928 and used as an intravenous anesthetic.
  • Sedative effect: reduction in CNS activity, leading to reduced anxiety and a calming effect.
  • Hypnotic effect: further reduction in CNS activity, leading to drowsiness and sleep.
  • Barbiturates can achieve both effects depending on the dose.

Why Barbiturates Are Dangerous

  • Increasing the dose of barbiturates leads to more CNS depression, which can result in coma, respiratory and vasomotor depression, and death.
  • This does not occur with benzodiazepines, making them safer alternatives.

Barbiturates Classification

Type Examples Duration T1/2
Ultra short acting Thiopental, Methohexital 30 min 8–10 h
Short acting Hexobarbital, Pentobarbital, Secobarbital 2 h 10–40 h
Intermediate acting Amobarbital, Butalbital 3–6 h 15–50 h
Long acting Phenobarbital, Primidone 6 h 80–120 h

Pharmacokinetics

  • Absorption: Oral formulations are rapidly absorbed with good bioavailability (e.g., phenobarbital ~90%).
  • Distribution: Highly lipid soluble, distribute to most body tissues including the brain, then rapidly redistribute to muscle and fat, decreasing plasma and brain concentrations. Redistribution terminates central effects.
  • Metabolism: Mainly in the liver by oxidation.
  • Excretion: Renal.

Mechanism of Action

  • Barbiturates act on GABAA receptors in the CNS.
  • GABA receptors are pentameric (five subunits) and are the chief inhibitory receptors in the CNS.
  • Normally activated by GABA neurotransmitter, leading to opening of chloride channels, reducing neuronal excitability and inhibiting nerve transmission (CNS inhibition).
  • Barbiturates enhance GABA action by interacting with alpha and beta subunits of GABAA receptors, increasing chloride ion flow, resulting in hyperpolarization and CNS depression.
  • Other sedative hypnotics (benzodiazepines, Z-drugs) also increase chloride flow via GABA receptors.

Pharmacologic Effects

  • Antianxiety effect at small doses.
  • Sedative and hypnotic effects at larger doses.
  • Anesthetic effect (thiopental, methohexital IV for short periods).
  • Euphoria.
  • Anticonvulsant effect (phenobarbital for tonic-clonic seizures).
  • Generalized CNS inhibition, including medullary centers in toxic doses (hypotension, bradycardia, apnea).
  • Induction of hepatic microsomal enzymes.

Therapeutic Uses

  • Phenobarbital: treatment of tonic-clonic seizures.
  • Phenobarbital: hepatic enzyme inducer for physiological jaundice in neonates.
  • Thiopental: induction of anesthesia (now replaced by propofol).
  • Thiopental: used for interrogation (requires more CNS activity to lie).
  • Methohexital: procedural anesthesia for cardioversion and fracture reduction.
  • Barbiturates were used for neonatal withdrawal, insomnia, and anxiety (now mostly replaced by benzodiazepines and newer hypnotics).

Adverse Effects

  • Tolerance and physical dependence (decreased GABA sensitivity, increased excitatory transmitters such as NMDA receptors).
  • Drowsiness and impaired concentration.
  • Hangover: psychomotor depression the day after use of long-acting drugs.
  • Respiratory and myocardial depression (especially IV forms).
  • Increased porphyrin synthesis (may precipitate acute intermittent porphyria).
  • Extravasation of thiopental can cause severe tissue necrosis (treated with local anesthetic and lidocaine injection).
  • Hepatotoxicity (especially with phenobarbital).

Drug Interactions

  • Phenobarbital is a strong CYP inducer: lowers plasma levels of other antiepileptics (lamotrigine, phenytoin, valproate), causes contraceptive failure, and affects other CYP-metabolized drugs.

Contraindications

  • Severe respiratory insufficiency.
  • Sleep apnea syndrome.
  • Acute narrow angle glaucoma (increases IOP).
  • Pregnancy (teratogenic).
  • Use with other CNS depressants (e.g., alcohol).

Toxicity

  • Barbiturates can be abused; overdose may occur intentionally or due to hepatic/renal problems.
  • Acute toxicity: CNS depression, respiratory failure, hemodynamic instability.

Treatment of Toxicity

  • No antidote available.
  • Mechanical ventilation.
  • IV fluids.
  • Alkalinization of urine (barbiturates are acidic drugs).
