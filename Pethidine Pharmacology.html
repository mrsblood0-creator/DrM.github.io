Pethidine: Mechanism of Action, Therapeutic Uses & Adverse Effects

History

  • Synthesized in 1939, by the German chemist Otto Eisleb, the intention was to create anticholinergic agent, but the invention worked as anticholinergic and also as analgesic
  • Meperidine was the first totally synthetic opioid
  • Opioid agonist with anti muscarinic effect opposite to morphine which had muscarinic effects

Overview

  • Meperidine is the scientific name, pethidine is the trade name
  • Synthetic opioid from the phenylpiperidine family (meperidine is the prototype agent of this family)
  • Compared to morphine, pethidine carries equal risk of addiction and it is more toxic because its metabolite (norpethidine) was found to have serotonergic effects so it leads to serotonin syndrome
  • One of the most commonly used opioids

Classification of opioids

Pharmacokinetics

  • Available as IM, sub cut, IV and oral syrup
  • Better absorbed orally and has greater bioavailability (50%) than morphine
  • Metabolized to norpethidine which is neurotoxic leading to activation of serotonin receptors (may lead to serotonin syndrome)
  • Excreted through liver and kidney, its half life is 8- 12 hours

Mechanism of action

  • Pethidine work as an opioid receptors especially mu opioids receptors in central nervous system and peripheral tissues and it work as agonist on these receptors
  • Pethidine has structural similarities to atropine, that is why it work also as anticholinergic

Pharmacologic effects

  • Analgesic potency is 10% of morphine
  • Activation of these opioid receptors lead to several central effects (CNS effects):
    • Analgesia
    • Euphoria
    • Sedation
    • Respiratory depression
    • Cough suppression
    • Nausea and vomiting
    • Pruritus
  • Activation of these opioid receptors lead to several peripheral effects including:
    • CVS
    • GIT: constipation
    • RS
    • Urinary system: may lead to urine retention
    • Immune: decrease immunity

Therapeutic uses

  • Meperidine used in Inferior MI because the patient usually has bradycardia and hypotension and meperidine has antimuscarinic activity
  • Used as analgesic in labor because it doesn’t prolong labor (little to no spasmogenic action) BUT its use decrease due to serotonin syndrome
  • Preferred in treating the pain associated with diverticulitis, because it decreases intestinal intraluminal pressure
  • Preferred in management of shivering during therapeutic hypothermia
  • Pethidine possess no advantageous effect on biliary spasm or renal colic in comparison with other opioids

Adverse effects

  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Urinary retention
  • Constipation
  • Less sedation compared to other opioids
  • It doesn’t cause miosis because of anticholinergic properties
  • Respiratory depression
  • Bronchoconstriction due to histamine release
  • Psychosis
  • It has weak atropine like action which lead to dry mouth, tachycardia
  • Tolerance
  • Physical dependence
  • Addiction
  • Serotonin syndrome: Pethidine risk of serotonin syndrome increase if used with SSRI or any other serotonergic drugs

Serotonin syndrome

  • Is a set of symptoms that occur with use of pethidine and other serotonergic drugs due to increase serotonin level in CNS
  • Symptoms include high BP, tachycardia, fever, agitation, hyperreflexia, tremor, sweating, mydriasis and diarrhea
  • Those symptoms range from mild to severe
  • Complications include seizures and rhabdomyolysis

Pethidine comparison against morphine

Morphine Pethidine
Chemistry: Natural opioid Synthetic opioid
Bioavailability: 25% 50%
Analgesic effect: Strong Weak (10% of morphine)
Autonomic effects: Vagal stimulation Anti cholinergic (opposite)
