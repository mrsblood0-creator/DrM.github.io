Buprenorphine: Mechanism of Action, Therapeutic Uses & Adverse Effects

Overview, pharmacokinetics, mechanism, effects, uses, and adverse effects of buprenorphine.

Overview

  • Buprenorphine is a synthetic opioid, working as a mixed agonist/antagonist (partial agonist).
  • Synthesized in the late 1960s, it is a derivative of thebaine.
  • Famous for its use in the treatment of pain and opioid misuse disorder.

Pharmacokinetics

  • Available in sublingual, buccal, transdermal, IV, and IM formulations.
  • Oral formula is not used due to significant first pass effect.
  • Bioavailability: sublingual 30%, buccal 65%.
  • Metabolized by the liver (CYP3A4 and CYP2C8).
  • Onset of action: 30 minutes after administration.
  • Elimination half-life: ~37 hours; duration of action: ~20 hours.
  • Excreted through kidney and liver.

Mechanism of Action

  • Acts as a potent partial agonist at mu opioid receptor and antagonist at kappa and delta receptors (mixed agonist antagonist).
  • Compared to morphine for equivalent dose, buprenorphine sublingual is ~50 times more potent, and 100 times more potent as a transdermal patch.
  • Can displace full mu agonists (e.g., morphine) due to higher potency, leading to withdrawal symptoms in opioid-dependent patients.

Pharmacologic Effect

  • Provides analgesia for moderate to severe pain, but can antagonize the analgesic effects of agonist opioids if used concurrently.
  • Less euphoric effects, thus lower abuse potential than agonist opioids.
  • Lower risk of respiratory depression compared to agonist opioids.
  • Slow dissociation from receptors leads to long duration of action and mild withdrawal symptoms when stopped.

Therapeutic Uses

  • Treatment of chronic pain (as transdermal patch), with long duration (~24 hours).
  • Treatment of opioid addiction (like methadone) due to long duration, less withdrawal symptoms, and blocking effects of agonist opioids during effect period.
  • Shorter and less severe withdrawal symptoms compared to methadone.
  • For opioid misuse disorder, used in combination with naloxone sublingually or buccally; IV use is avoided to prevent abuse (naloxone would cause acute withdrawal if injected).

Adverse Effects

  • Does not cause respiratory depression unless at very high dose, but if it occurs, it cannot be reversed by naloxone.
  • Risk of respiratory depression is higher if injected IV with benzodiazepines.
  • Increased blood pressure due to increased systemic and pulmonary vascular resistance.
  • Prolongation of QT interval (risky in cardiovascular diseases).
  • Psychotomimetic effects: hallucinations, nightmares, anxiety (reported with mixed agonist-antagonist drugs).
  • Low addiction risk due to less mu activation.
  • Nausea and vomiting.
  • Constipation.
  • Headache, mild amnesia, cognitive and neural inhibition.
  • Pruritus.
  • Risk of seizures in epilepsy patients.
  • Miosis.
  • Urinary retention.
