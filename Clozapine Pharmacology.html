Clozapine Pharmacology: Mechanism of Action, Therapeutic Uses & Adverse Effects

Overview

  • Famous trade names: Clozaril and Leponex
  • It is atypical antipsychotic medication (new, second generation) and the first atypical to be discovered
  • It is mainly used in treatment of schizophrenia and schizoaffective disorder in resistant cases
  • Clozapine was synthesized in 1958 by the Swiss pharmaceutical company Wander AG

Pharmacokinetics

  • Available as oral formula only
  • It is well absorbed with bioavailability of 60% due to first pass metabolism
  • Metabolized extensively by the liver via CYP enzymes, especially CYP1A2 and excreted through urine and feces
  • Elimination half life is 14 hours

Mechanism of action

  • clozapine antagonizes serotonin 5HT receptors and dopamine receptors
  • Serotonin antagonism makes clozapine good in managing the negative symptoms of schizophrenia, like social isolation, poverty of speech, lack of motivation and inability to feel pleasure
  • Antagonism of serotonin by this medication also lead to relief of some of the extrapyramidal side effects that occur as a result of dopamine antagonism
  • BUT serotonin antagonism lead to weight gain and metabolic changes as adverse effects with the use of this medication
  • Clozapine antagonizes dopamine but it has higher affinity to dopamine D4 receptors more than D2 receptors which lead to less extrapyramidal symptoms compared to other antipsychotics
  • Dopamine antagonism in the mesolimbic and mesocortical pathway which lead to relieving of the positive symptoms of schizophrenia
  • It also blocks dopamine in the tuberoinfundibular pathway but it has lower risk of hyperprolactinemia compared with other antipsychotics
  • It has anti histamine activity which lead to drowsiness
  • It also block muscarinic receptors including M1, 2, 3, 5; it is agonist at M4
  • It blocks alpha adrenergic receptors to some degree too

Therapeutic uses

  • Use of clozapine in treatment of schizophrenia has shown better symptoms control and reduced risk of hospitalization and drug discontinuation but it is not used as a first line because it may lead to agranulocytosis and GIT hypomotility which may kill the patient (that will be discussed in the adverse effects part of this article)
  • It is used in treatment of schizophrenia and schizoaffective disorder in:
    • Patients who didn’t have adequate response to two other antipsychotic medications (resistant cases)
    • Patients who are unable to tolerate other antipsychotics due to extrapyramidal side effects
    • Patients who develop tardive dyskinesia while on some other antipsychotic medication
  • It is used in treatment of psychosis that occur as a side effect to Parkinson’s disease treatment
  • It is used for reducing risk of suicide in people with schizophrenia or schizoaffective disorder who has high suicide risk
  • Third line in treatment of manic phase of bipolar disorder (lithium being first line, other atypicals being second line)

Dosage

  • According to FDA, max dose is 900 mg/day, average dose is 300 mg/day for women and 400 mg/day for men

Adverse effects

  • Clozapine used as a second line because it may lead to agranulocytosis (agranulocytosis likely to be fatal) in the first year and a half of treatment, after that risk reduces, also because it lead to GIT hypomotility (surprisingly most common cause of death from this medication more than agranulocytosis even)
  • clozapine lead to bone marrow suppression and agranulocytosis (1% of cases) which is severe neutropenia less than 500 /mm
  • Risk factors for agranulocytosis are old age, female, concurrent tx with medications that cause same condition, monitor with CBC
  • It may lead to GIT hypomotility which may lead to severe constipation, fecal impaction, paralytic ileus, bowel obstruction, acute megacolon, ischemia or necrosis
  • It may lead to myocarditis (3% of cases) which is inflammation of the heart muscle, also fatal
  • Risk factors for myocarditis include rapid titration of the drug, concurrent use of SSRIs, illicit substance abuse
  • Most common adverse effect of clozapine is hypersalivation in 60% of the patients, clozapine is agonist in M4 which is highly expressed in salivary glands
  • Weight gain, high blood sugar levels due to serotonin antagonism
  • It also block alpha adrenergic receptors to some degree leading to postural hypotension and palpitation
  • Increased risk of death if used in patient with dementia related psychosis
  • Extrapyramidal symptoms are not common with this medication because of its anticholinergic effects and serotonin antagonism and its higher affinity for dopamine D4 more than D2 receptors
  • Increase risk of seizures in seizure disorders patients
  • Anticholinergic side effects: Elevated temperature, sedation, constipation and urinary retention
  • Periorbital edema (rare)

Clozapine withdrawal

  • Abrupt withdrawal of clozapine lead to cholinergic rebound effects, those include
    • Indigestion
    • Diarrhea
    • Nausea/vomiting
    • Hypersalivation
    • Sweating
    • Insomnia
    • Agitation

Drug interactions

  • Fluvoxamine (SSRI), theophylline, ciprofloxacin all inhibit metabolism of the medication leading to increased blood levels and overdose
  • Carbamazepine increase metabolism of this medication leading to low plasma levels and treatment failure and it also increase risk of agranulocytosis

Clozapine overdose

  • Symptoms of overdose include sedation, confusion, tachycardia, seizures and ataxia
