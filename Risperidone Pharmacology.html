Risperidone Pharmacology: Mechanism of Action, Therapeutic Uses & Adverse Effects

Overview

  • Famous trade name is Risperdal
  • It is atypical antipsychotic medication (new, second generation)
  • It is used in treatment of schizophrenia and some other mental disorders and it is better tolerated than typical antipsychotics because of fewer side effects
  • Risperidone synthesized in 1980 in Belgium

Pharmacokinetics

  • Risperidone is available as oral, IM, and IV formulas, there is long acting injectables with duration of action of 2-4 weeks
  • Long acting formulas provide improved compliance and reduced relapse rates compared to oral formulas
  • Oral formula of the risperidone has good absorption with bioavailability of 70%
  • It is metabolized by the liver by CYP enzymes especially CYP2D6
  • Excreted mainly through urine and it also excreted through feces

Mechanism of Action

  • Risperidone antagonizes serotonin 5HT receptors and dopamine D2 receptors
  • BUT it has more serotonin antagonism than dopamine antagonism and that what it make different from typical antipsychotics
  • Serotonin antagonism makes risperidone good in managing the negative symptoms of schizophrenia, like social isolation, poverty of speech, lack of motivation and inability to feel pleasure
  • Antagonism of serotonin by this medication also lead to relief of some of the extrapyramidal side effects that occur as a result of dopamine antagonism
  • BUT serotonin antagonism lead to weight gain and metabolic changes as adverse effects with the use of this medication
  • Risperidone work by antagonizing dopamine D2 receptors in the brain
  • It blocks dopamine in the mesolimbic and mesocortical pathway which lead to relieving of the psychosis
  • It also blocks dopamine in the nigrostriatal pathway which lead to extrapyramidal symptoms as adverse effects
  • It also blocks dopamine in the tuberoinfundibular pathway lead to hyperprolactinemia as an adverse effect
  • It has anti histamine activity which lead to drowsiness
  • It blocks alpha adrenergic receptors to some degree too

FDA Approved Therapeutic Uses

  • Schizophrenia (in adults and children aged 13 and older): risperidone is more effective in treating schizophrenia than all typical antipsychotics except for haloperidol
  • Acute manic or mixed episodes of bipolar disorder: as monotherapy for adults and children aged 10 and older
  • Acute manic or mixed episodes of bipolar disorder: as adjunctive with lithium or valproate
  • Autism in children aged 5 and older: risperidone controls problematic behaviors, aggression, self injury and rapid mood changes

Non FDA Approved Therapeutic Uses

  • Dementia: they produce slight benefits in dementia patients, but there is increase risk of death (because it increase risk of stroke)
  • Psychotic polydipsia
  • PTSD, delirium, depression, borderline personality, conduct disorder and Tourette disorder

Dosing

  • For schizophrenia: start with 1 mg daily then increase the dose by 0.5 mg every six to seven days to target 2 mg daily, doses exceeding 4 mg per day are rarely more effective, max dose is 16 mg per day; for elderly same dose except you increase by 0.25 mg every six to seven days instead of 0.5 mg
  • For acute manic episodes of bipolar disorder: 2 to 3 mg daily dose, max dose is 6 mg per day
  • Daily dose in both disorders can be taken at once or divided into two doses

Adverse Effects

  • Weight gain, high blood sugar levels and metabolic problems
  • Sedation
  • Hyperprolactinemia lead to sexual dysfunction
  • Increase risk of seizures in seizure disorders patients
  • Extrapyramidal symptoms (movement disorders): risperidone produce more extrapyramidal symptoms than other atypical antipsychotics does
  • Acute dystonia: muscle spasm develops within hours to days after initiation
  • Akathisia: restlessness develops days to months after initiation
  • Tardive dyskinesia: uncontrollable repetitive movements develops after years of initiation
  • Muscle rigidity
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome
  • Muscle rigidity
  • Hyperthermia
  • Autonomic disturbance

Warning

There is increased risk of death if haloperidol is used in elderly patient with dementia related psychosis, that is why it is not FDA approved for this use

Contraindications

  • Previous hypersensitivity reaction to the risperidone or paliperidone (risperidone metabolite)
