Chloramphenicol Pharmacology

Introduction

Chloramphenicol is a broad-spectrum antibiotic originally derived from Streptomyces venezuelae. It is now produced synthetically and is used to treat serious infections where other antibiotics are ineffective or contraindicated.

Mechanism of Action

  • Inhibits bacterial protein synthesis by binding to the 50S ribosomal subunit.
  • Prevents peptide bond formation by inhibiting peptidyl transferase activity.
  • Bacteriostatic, but can be bactericidal against some organisms (e.g., Haemophilus influenzae, Neisseria meningitidis).

Spectrum of Activity

  • Effective against Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria.
  • Active against anaerobes, Rickettsia, and Chlamydia.
  • Not effective against Pseudomonas aeruginosa and some Enterobacteriaceae.

Pharmacokinetics

  • Well absorbed orally; also available as IV and topical formulations.
  • Widely distributed, including cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
  • Metabolized in the liver by glucuronidation.
  • Excreted primarily by the kidneys.
  • Half-life: 1.5â€“4 hours (may be prolonged in neonates or liver dysfunction).

Clinical Uses

  • Serious infections: meningitis, typhoid fever, rickettsial infections, brain abscess.
  • Reserved for cases where safer alternatives are ineffective or contraindicated.
  • Topical use in eye infections (conjunctivitis).

Adverse Effects

  • Bone marrow suppression (dose-related and idiosyncratic aplastic anemia).
  • Gray baby syndrome (in neonates due to immature liver enzymes).
  • Gastrointestinal disturbances.
  • Hypersensitivity reactions.

Contraindications & Precautions

  • Use with caution in neonates, pregnant women, and patients with liver dysfunction.
  • Monitor blood counts during prolonged therapy.
